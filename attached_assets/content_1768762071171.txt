FAQ Items:
Q1: How do I know which service I need?
A1:
 It depends on your stakeholders' requirements. Banks typically require reviews or audits. Early-stage companies often start with compilations. If you're unsure, schedule a consultation and we'll help you determine the appropriate engagement level based on your specific situation.

Q2: How long does an assurance engagement take?
A2:
 Compilations typically take 2-3 weeks, reviews 3-5 weeks, and audits 6-10 weeks from information request to final report delivery. Timeline depends on your responsiveness to information requests and complexity of your financials.

Q3: What financial statements are included?
A3:
 Standard engagements include Balance Sheet, Income Statement, Statement of Cash Flows, and footnote disclosures. We prepare statements in accordance with US GAAP or other applicable accounting frameworks.

Q4: Do you handle nonprofit audits or single audits?
A4:
 Yes. We perform audits for nonprofit organizations, including single audits (Uniform Guidance) for entities with federal grant expenditures over $750,000. Pricing varies based on grant complexity.

Q5: Can you audit our internal controls (SOX compliance)?
A5:
 While we don't perform SOX 404 attestations (public company requirement), we do evaluate internal controls as part of financial statement audits and can provide management letters with control recommendations. For SOC 2 compliance, see our Risk Advisory services.

Q6: What if we have complex accounting issues?
A6:
 We handle revenue recognition (ASC 606), lease accounting (ASC 842), stock-based compensation, and other technical areas. Complex situations may require additional scoping, which we discuss during engagement planning.

Q7: Do you work with our existing accounting team?
A7:
 Yes. We collaborate directly with your CFO, controller, or accounting staff throughout the engagement. We aim to be an extension of your team, not a disruption.

SECTION 7 ‚Äî FAQ
Q1: What happens if I ignore an IRS notice?
A1:
 Ignoring IRS notices escalates the situation. The IRS can assess additional penalties, file liens, levy your bank accounts, or garnish wages. Every notice has a response deadline‚Äîit's critical to act quickly.
Q2: Can you stop a wage garnishment or bank levy?
A2:
 Yes. We can request levy releases and negotiate alternative collection methods like installment agreements. Time is critical‚Äîcontact us immediately if you've received a levy notice.
Q3: What is an Offer in Compromise?
A3:
 An OIC allows you to settle your tax debt for less than the full amount owed if you meet specific financial hardship criteria. We evaluate your eligibility and prepare the application if appropriate.
Q4: How long does tax resolution take?
A4:
 Simple notice responses: 2-4 weeks. Audits: 3-6 months. Complex resolution (OIC, multiple years): 6-12 months. Timeline varies based on IRS workload and case complexity.
Q5: Do I need to speak with the IRS myself?
A5:
 No. Once we file Power of Attorney (Form 2848), all IRS communication goes through us. You won't need to speak with IRS agents directly.
Q6: What if I haven't filed taxes in years?
A6:
 We can prepare prior-year returns and negotiate resolution for both filing and payment obligations. The IRS typically requires 6 years of back returns before considering collection alternatives.

SECTION 7 ‚Äî FAQ
Q1: When do I need a 409A valuation?
A1:
 You need a 409A before issuing your first stock options and then: (1) at least every 12 months, (2) within 30 days of a material event (fundraise, M&A offer, major milestone), or (3) when your prior 409A expires.
Q2: How long is a 409A valid?
A2:
 409A valuations are valid for 12 months OR until a material event occurs (whichever comes first). Material events include fundraising, significant revenue changes, or M&A offers.
Q3: What information do you need from us?
A3:
 We need: current cap table, recent financial statements (past 2 years + projections), recent fundraising documents (if any), and company overview (business model, products, market).
Q4: Can we expedite the process?
A4:
 Yes. For an additional fee, we can deliver in 3-5 business days. Contact us to discuss expedited timing.
Q5: What valuation methods do you use?
A5:
 We use the Option Pricing Method (OPM) or Probability-Weighted Expected Return Method (PWERM) depending on your stage. We may also consider market multiples and discounted cash flow analysis.
Q6: Will this affect my next fundraising valuation?
A6:
 No. 409A values common stock for option pricing. Preferred stock (what investors buy) is valued differently and typically higher due to liquidation preferences and other rights.

SECTION 8 ‚Äî FAQ
Q1: Can non-US residents own a US company?
A1:
 Yes. There are no citizenship or residency requirements to form and own a US LLC or C-Corp. You don't need a US address, SSN, or visa.
Q2: Which state should I incorporate in?
A2:
 Delaware for C-Corps (industry standard, VC-preferred). Delaware or Wyoming for LLCs (favorable laws, privacy protections). We'll guide you based on your situation.
Q3: Can I open a US bank account remotely?
A3:
 Yes. Mercury, Brex, and Relay offer remote account opening for US entities. We provide application support and introduce you to these banking partners.
Q4: Do I need to visit the US to set this up?
A4:
 No. The entire formation process is remote. Bank accounts can also be opened remotely with digital-first banks.
Q5: What ongoing compliance is required?
A5:
 Delaware requires an annual report ($300/year for corps, $300/year for LLCs) plus registered agent fees. You'll also need to file federal tax returns annually. We can handle ongoing compliance if needed.
Q6: How is a US entity taxed?
A6:
 LLCs: typically pass-through (no entity-level tax). C-Corps: 21% federal corporate tax. If you're a non-US resident owner with no US operations, you may have minimal US tax obligations. We provide tax consultation to explain your specific situation.
